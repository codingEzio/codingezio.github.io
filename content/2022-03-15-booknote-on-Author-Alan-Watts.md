### *Become What You Are*

> If we allow our thoughts, the past, the present and the future ones, to link up in a series, we put ourself under restraint. On the other hand, if we let our mind attach to nothing at all times and towards all things we gain emancipation.

> **A man does not really begin to be alive until he has lost himself, until he has released the anxious grasp which he normally holds upon his life, his property, his reputation and position.**

> The words about finding one’s life through losing it were not really a precept that could simply be practiced and obeyed. This is what makes all the talk about the necessity of selflessness or the task of transcending the ego so fantastically misunderstood. Treated as a precept, it makes for every kind of moral and spiritual phoniness.
>> Always have a large pinch of salt handy when you meet the fellow who talks about trying to renounce himself, to overcome his ego.

> For the selfishness of the self thrives on the notion that it can command itself, that it is the lord and master of its own processes, of its own motives and desires. Thus the one important result of any really serious attempt at self-renunciation or self-acceptance is the humiliating discovery that it is impossible.
>> And this precisely is that death to oneself which is the improbable source of a way of life so new and so alive that it feels like having been born again.

### *Out of Your Mind*

> The two fundamental images of the world that we’ve been operating under for more than two thousand years are essentially models of the universe: the *ceramic* model and the *fully automatic* model.
>
> The ceramic model of the universe originates from the book of Genesis, from which Judaism, Christianity, and Islam all derive their basic picture of the world. The world is *an artifact made by the Creator* — just as a potter *forms pots out of clay*, or a carpenter *fashions tables and chairs from wood*.
>
> Just as the potter takes clay and imposes his will upon it, so does the Creator craft the universe out of this fundamental stuff.
>
> - the clay itself is formness and comes with no intelligence
> - requires an external intelligence to bring it to life
> - so we *are*, just like artifacts, *made* by some kind of God

> "How was I made?"
>> When you make something, you *put it together* — you arrange its parts, you work from the *outside to the in*.

> "How did I grow?"
>> Growth happens in the opposite direction—that is, from the *inside to the out*. It's something *expands*, *burgeons*, *blossoms*, and happens all over itself at once.

> "What is *matter*?"
>> .. is one they stopped asking long ago. See, in exploring the nature of matter, physicists realized they could only describe it in terms of *behavior* in terms of *form and pattern*. In finding *smaller and smaller* particles—atoms, electrons, protons, all sorts of nuclear particles—you never arrive at any *fundamental* stuff, so you can only describe *how it appears to act*.
>>
> We use the word *stuff* because that’s how the world looks when our eyes are out of focus. We think of stuff as if it were some kind of *undifferentiated goo*, but that’s merely because *our vision is fuzzy*. When we focus, we’re able to see forms and patterns, and **all we can really talk about is patterns**. The picture of the world offered by the most sophisticated efforts of physics today is **not one of formed stuff or potted clay, but patterns—self-moving, self-designing, dancing patterns**. But our common sense hasn’t yet caught up with this new picture.
